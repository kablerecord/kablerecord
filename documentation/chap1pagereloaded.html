The El Paso Disaster

Hour 78 and Counting

The Setup

What started as a routine concrete job in El Paso became a 78-hour nightmare that would define everything that came after. When the hydraulic line burst at 2 AM, covering everything in fluid and making the massive Thor slab immovable, most contractors would have called it a loss.

The Crisis Moment:

  • The Thor Slab: 12x24 feet of reinforced concrete, impossible to move by hand
  • Destroyed Equipment: Hydraulic fluid had contaminated everything
  • Ticking Clock: Monday deadline with severe penalties for delays
  • Backup Plan Failed: Even the backup backhoe bucket couldn't handle the slab
  • 2 AM Decision: Either quit and lose everything, or find a way

From the Book: The Moment Everything Changed

While we were grossly inexperienced and in way over our heads, we were‚Äîare‚Äîgood at figuring things out. So I started wielding that useless hammer like a battle axe. I'd lift it high in the air, jam the controls down, and smash it into the concrete with every pound of force I could squeeze out of that backhoe. Lift. BANG. Lift. BANG.

The other operator watched for any crack, any weakness. When I'd create the tiniest gap, he'd wedge his bucket in and pry with everything he had. Two guys turning construction equipment into medieval siege weapons because the modern tools had failed us.

At some point late into the night, we peeled the three-foot backhoe bucket apart like a banana. The teeth and the bottom of it ripping the welds as it folded back. Now all we had was one 12" bucket.

No one was coming to save us. We were in another town on a Saturday. No rental places open. No backup equipment available. Just us, two undersized backhoes, and 3,000 square feet of concrete that needed to be replaced by Monday morning.

The 2 AM Decision That Changes Everything

What I'm Really Trying to Tell You

Here's the thing about that moment at 2 AM...

It wasn't about concrete. It wasn't about construction. It wasn't even about finishing the job.

It was about discovering that the story you tell yourself in crisis becomes the story of your life.

From the book: "Faith and doubt are both beliefs. In that moment at 2 AM, covered in hydraulic fluid and concrete dust, while staring at a disaster that wasn't my fault but absolutely was my problem, I could have believed one of two stories:"

Story #1:

"This is impossible. We're in over our heads. No one could fix this."

Story #2:

"We're going to finish this. We'll figure it out. We always do."

"At that moment, both were equally true. The difference was which story I chose to tell myself."

Most people choose Story #1. It's logical. It's reasonable. It's what any sane person would do.

But builders choose Story #2.

Not because we're crazy (though that helps). Not because we're stronger or smarter or better.

Because we understand something most people don't:

Your character isn't what you do when life is easy. It's who you become when everything goes wrong.
